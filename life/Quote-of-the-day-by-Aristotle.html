Quote of the day by Aristotle: 'Whosoever is delighted in solitude is either a wild beast or a God'

"No man is an island. Greek philosopher " had realised this truth thousands of years ago. In his seminal work Politics, he wrote, “Whosoever is delighted in solitude is either a wild beast or a God.” This simple observation sums up a timeless truth about humanity—no matter how self-sufficient we think ourselves to be, as human beings, we do crave companionship, and having a solid support system is a blessing.

His reflection captures a universal truth: although many claim self-reliance, instinctively seeks companionship, emotional security, and shared belonging. A dependable network of people enriches our lives and shapes our inner world in ways solitude cannot replicate.

Aristotle’s Larger Vision of Community

Aristotle’s broader reflections on society help explain this complex relationship between the individual and the collective. He famously wrote that society emerges before the formation of any single person, emphasizing that our personalities, values, and entire sense of identity are moulded by the groups we grow up in. This view contradicts today’s strong focus on individual achievement, urging us to recognize that those who genuinely enjoy pure isolation might stand at the extremes of existence—either entirely beyond human needs or stripped of them.

To Aristotle, a community was not merely a gathering of autonomous people choosing to cooperate. Instead, he believed that social structures were fundamental conditions of human life, existing prior to individuals and shaping their character through shared customs, communication, and moral learning.

Why Isolation Appeared Unnatural to Him

Because of this understanding, Aristotle considered complete detachment from society contrary to human nature. A person raised outside a community, he argued, would miss essential qualities that define humanity. Foundational abilities such as speech, moral judgment, and rational thinking develop only through interaction with others. Without communal life, these capabilities would remain unformed, preventing a person from becoming fully human.

The Life and Intellectual Journey of Aristotle

Despite his immense influence, historians possess only limited information about Aristotle’s personal life. He was born in Stagira, a northern Greek city, during the Classical era. Losing his father, Nicomachus, at a young age, he grew up under the care of a guardian. At around eighteen, he travelled to Athens to study at Plato’s Academy, remaining there for nearly two decades. After Plato’s death, he left Athens, and soon after, King Philip II of Macedon invited him to tutor the young Alexander, later known as Alexander the Great. Aristotle eventually founded a library at the Lyceum, enabling him to write extensively on papyrus scrolls.

Lasting Global Influence

Although he produced hundreds of works, only a portion survives today, many of which were not created for public reading. His writings synthesized earlier philosophical traditions and shaped fields ranging from logic to ethics. Across centuries—from medieval Europe to Islamic scholarship—his ideas guided scientific thought and theological debate. His influence persisted up to the Renaissance and only gradually gave way during the Enlightenment, when new scientific frameworks emerged.
